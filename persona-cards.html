Problem Statement

3D printer owners can print almost anything — but only if the file already exists. When a part breaks, doesn't fit, or simply isn't available, the natural solution is to make it.

But getting from a clear mental image of what's needed to a printable file requires learning professional modeling software — a significant commitment that most people have no reason to make beyond a single immediate need.

Most people don't make that trade. They give up, pay someone else, or wait. The gap between knowing what you need and being able to make it is the opportunity.

Personas

R
Roger, 31 — The Printer Owner
Primary Persona
Owns a 3D printer 1–3 years printing Prints weekly Self-described intermediate
Story

Roger is a hands-on hobbyist — RC trucks, bike mods, home projects. He's owned a 3D printer for about two years and uses it every week. He's comfortable with the hardware and knows his way around a slicer. Printing isn't the hard part.

The hard part is when he needs something that doesn't exist yet. A shock tower that snapped on a discontinued model. A mount that fits an awkward gap. A bracket sized exactly right. Every time he tries to figure it out, he lands on the same answer: learn CAD. And every time, it feels like the wrong trade.

He's not against learning. He just doesn't want to become a modeler. He wants the part.

Needs & Goals
  • Create a custom or replacement part without learning a full modeling workflow
  • Keep their hobby moving without a months-long detour into new software
  • Get from "I know exactly what I need" to a file they can print
Challenges
  • Every existing path to a custom part assumes you want to learn CAD — not just get the result
  • The learning investment feels out of proportion to the ask
  • When something breaks or doesn't fit, there's no quick way forward
S
Sam, 42 — The Access User
Secondary Persona
No printer ownership Library / makerspace / service One-time need
Story

Sam doesn't own a printer. They found out their local library has one — or stumbled across a print-on-demand service — while trying to solve a specific problem. A broken appliance knob. A camera mount that doesn't exist in the right size. Something they need once.

They've figured out the printing side. What they can't figure out is the file. Every search leads to the same place: a modeling tutorial they don't have time for, or a freelancer quote that costs more than the original product.

The wall isn't about hardware. It's that the only path to a file they can use runs through a skill they'll never need again.

Needs & Goals
  • Solve the immediate problem and move on
  • Get a printable file without learning a tool they'll use once
Challenges
  • No ongoing context to justify learning CAD — the need is one-time
  • Existing workarounds (freelancers, communities) add time, cost, or uncertainty
  • The gap between "I know what I need" and "I have a file" has no accessible bridge
Roger hits this wall repeatedly — his hobby continuously generates new needs. Sam hits it once, hard, and gives up or finds a workaround. Roger is a retained user. Sam is an acquisition story.
